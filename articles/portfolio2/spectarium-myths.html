Myths

Spectarium Games · Level Design & Tools · sole LD

A mobile ARPG built around hand-stitched dungeons. I owned the level-design pipeline end-to-end across three biomes — building the zones, the tools used to build them, and the procedural-content roadmap that was going to scale it.

  • Unity
  • 3 biomes shipped
  • ~150 zones authored
  • Tool pipeline
  • PCG roadmap

Case study

Problem

Myths was a live-service ARPG where the player’s entire loop sat inside dungeons that take four to six minutes to clear. That cadence is brutal on a content pipeline. Every dungeon is one of the first things a returning player sees, and reuse is noticed within minutes.

Three problems kept stacking up:

  • Throughput — with every zone made by hand, output was gated by headcount.
  • Value per hour — a reused zone returned a player to content they had already seen, so each authoring hour yielded exactly one hour of new play.
  • Workflow — layout, decoration, encounters and dungeon assembly were all manual. The throughput was too low and the wrist strain was real. I’d rather generate content than generate carpal-tunnel issues.

Approach

The team converged on a tile-based zone system, with each biome carrying its own constraints and its own ruleset. Zones were assembled into dungeons via scriptable objects, and a tool pipeline cut authoring friction at every step.

+

Zones — one anatomy, three biomes

Every zone went through the same pipeline: blockout → tile layout (plus blockers) → encounters → testing → decoration → custom navmesh → playtest. Each zone has a visual mesh, a walkable mesh for the client, and a navmesh shared by client and server.

Where biomes diverged was in the tile system that filled the blockout:

+

Norse — outdoor, directional, baked into the vertices

Tile sizes of 5×5, 10×10, 10×20 and 20×20. Norse needed long directional shadows but baked light was out (zones stitched together at runtime so shadow seams broke at every border) and dynamic light was out (mobile budget).

The solution was to vertex-paint shadows into the tiles themselves. Each tile got extra geometry to support it, and since a tile could land in four rotations we needed four authored variants per tile — one for 0°, 90°, 180° and 270°. I built a small tool that auto-swapped tiles to the correct lighting variant as they rotated.

A small escape valve: free-floating shadows under trees that could cross zone borders, since they had no colliders and didn’t affect gameplay.

Drag to reveal the underlying tile + lighting setup.
+

Mayan — interior, narrow, camera-aware

Floor tiles at 2×2 with walls authored at the 2-unit anchor, world-position texturing on floors so seams disappeared. Fewer building pieces than Norse and a much more claustrophobic feel.

The new problem here was visibility. The camera couldn’t rotate, but interior walls naturally block sightlines. Three systems shared the load: a cutout around the player, a red silhouette overlay for enemies behind walls, and walls split into a top piece and a short “stub” bottom piece so the camera could fade the top out without losing the player’s read of the floor plan.

Mayan interior — freestanding rotatable walls, worldspace floor texturing.
+

Watcher — bright interior, more freedom per tile

The last biome and the home of the FTUE. Same general rules as Mayan, but with a bigger variety of floor tiles that did not rely on worldspace texturing. That gave us deliberate per-tile patterning — tile-stones, mosaics — and a trim concept for the seams where floors met walls.

Watcher biome — brighter, more decorative per-tile work.

Across the three biomes I shipped around 150 zones. Decoration leaned on compositions (collections of prefabs) that could be dropped into a zone as a unit; the long-term direction was to standardize them per biome so we could swap sub-themes without reauthoring layouts.

Encounters had three modes already running: manual placement for set-pieces, point-based dynamic spawn with a per-point enemy list for FTUE-style flow, and even-density dynamic spawning across a whole dungeon for general combat. The roadmap added a dungeon graph on top of that to vary density per area.

+

Dungeons — scriptable assembly

A dungeon was just a sequence of zones with a starter and an exit, with regular, scenario and adapter zones between them — adapters bridging gaps where zone borders didn’t match. Dungeons were hand-assembled inside a scriptable object, with a visual tool that read the prefab positions relative to the starter zone and emitted the scriptable object directly. Meta-fields on the dungeon (weather, biome) rode along.

Long term, the majority of these would have been generated from dungeon graph templates — covered in the next section.

+

The procedural-content roadmap

A while back I put together a presentation for the team laying out a plan for how to shift our dungeon content from fully hand-built to a more procedural workflow. The original was meant to spark a discussion, so it leans into trade-offs rather than absolutes — the version below pulls those thoughts together as a written walkthrough of how I’d approach scaling content delivery for a live-service game.

Two paths

Two directions came out of this, both valid with different trade-offs.

Variants are the safer bet given our constraints — a natural extension of what we already had, retaining current quality and squeezing additional value out of each zone for comparatively little extra work. This is the path I’d push for, and most of what follows is about how it would work.

Fully generated is the alternative — generating rooms or dungeons in one go. More rigid, more upfront investment, but huge long-term value across the project and likely future ones. Worth prototyping in parallel for parts of the game rather than betting the project on it now.

Dungeon graph

The graph sits at the top of the hierarchy and rains down over every other aspect of the dungeon. Each node is a zone with constraints — a theme (or a list of acceptable themes), an encounter density, whether it’s optional or mandatory, fixed or varying — and the graph randomises order and contents within those rules. This is where partial control lives: force two connected zones to host a scenario, lock a starting biome, guarantee a miniboss appears before an exit, while still letting the rest shuffle. Story or scenario beats can be steered from here too.

Decoration hierarchy

Decoration is where most of the time goes, alongside chasing elusive navmesh issues. At the time everything lived as compositions or freestanding props, which is too flat. Splitting it into three layers, each with a different level of control:

Variants

Variants are the big zone-level pieces — a whole wall plus its surroundings, a fancy centerpiece, a pit of gas clouds. The highest level of variable content and where theme control bites: two variants for “ritualistic,” two for “ornate,” two for “rubble.” The dungeon graph picks the theme; the zone picks one of the matching variants.

Large chunks control the majority of a zone’s look, varied per theme.

Compositions

Compositions are what variants are mainly built from. We already used them, but the key shift is standardised sizes (S, M, L, XL) with distinct shapes for corners, walls and central standalone pieces. That makes them swappable without trouble: a variant picks “ritual site” with a sacrificial stone, and a randomiser can swap the stone for a statue and shuffle the surrounding props without touching colliders.

Wall and inner-corner compositions, built to slot into the same anchor points.
Outer-corner and freestanding variants, swappable at the right size class.

Micro details

Micro details are the smallest decorative aspects — props of certain kinds, ideally part of a composition or standalone. Randomisable per run with no impact on colliders or navmesh: pebble swaps, bones spawning or not, lanterns on or off. Cheap variety once the setup is in place.

Uncontrolled changes — zero effort after setup.

Encounters and events

Hand-placed encounters carry little value for our combat style and break easily: designers update visuals without re-checking enemies, and there is no zone editor showing both at once. Default to auto-spread enemy clusters per zone, keep directed combat for scenarios. Bosses and tutorials still manual.

Auto-spread clusters with directed encounters reserved for scenarios and bosses.


Three tiers of events. Scenarios are fixed zones with manual setup, objects to interact with that trigger a challenge and grant a reward. Some can share a zone visually, and the dungeon graph lets us force two of them into connected zones. Minor gameplay events are algorithm-driven: a random enemy ambush, a treasure-goblin spawn. Traps probably live here too. Visual events are flavour moments with no mechanical impact — dust raining from a rubble pile, a squirrel crossing the path, the shadow of something large rushing across the screen. Most likely client-side.

Technical considerations

Loose threads that don’t fit neatly into the plan above but shape what is actually feasible.

Navmesh and colliders are part of the game config and a good chunk of why a build takes time. Could we checksum them and skip rebuilds when unchanged?

Level data — if we end up with 500 zones and 10,000 levels, do we hit a storage or distribution wall? Storing seeds instead of fully generated data might be worth it.

Dungeon pool — tied to the previous point. If storage really is a problem, we could rotate a pool of dungeons every X days.

Zone editing tool — needed to inspect gameplay setup alongside zone geometry. Am I overlapping a gameplay event with a collider? Is a monster getting stuck in a wall? Hard to answer without the tool.

Eval and QA — more randomisation makes QA harder. We’d likely need a vetting process before pushing dungeons live.

+

Tools that paid for themselves

Most of the tooling was small scripts wired to one task each — snapping, rotating, automatically swapping out tiles for their lighting-variant siblings. A zone setup tool generated the meta-data scaffolding for new zones; the same idea for dungeons. With those scripts running, I could lay out a 100×100m zone in around five minutes.

Macro pad

For pure efficiency I worked off a layered macro pad mapping my most-used tools and shortcuts. In Myths specifically it laid out tile placement by rotation in a numpad shape — one key per rotation per common tile size. Strongly recommend this for any level designer who would like to keep their wrists.

Asset palette

A preview-then-place tool: spread a selection of assets across the scene, audition the composition, and only commit once it reads correctly. Saved a lot of redo for prop-dense decorations.

Result

Around 150 zones shipped across three biomes. 100×100m zone layouts in roughly five minutes thanks to the macro pad plus the auto-tile-swap tooling. Two of five planned biomes completed end-to-end with their bosses, with the Watcher biome carrying the FTUE. The procedural-content roadmap was fully documented and ready to execute.

Reflection

The Norse vertex-painted-shadow system was the right answer to the constraints we had, but in hindsight I’d push back harder on those constraints earlier — the tile authoring cost compounded across the project. I’d also start the dungeon-graph work earlier so the first biome ships on top of it rather than retrofitting later.

The project ended before destructibles, traps and PCG dungeons shipped — the company ran into financial trouble before that part of the roadmap could land. The plans for what came next live in the section above.

Houdini & Cliffs 02.02.2021

A few years ago, as a level designer at Sandbox Interactive, I felt how inefficient our initial world-building pipeline was. Nearly every rock and tree was placed by hand - one click meant one asset placed. While we eventually optimized the process, a considerable amount of time and resources had already been spent manually building the environment.

Looking back, I realize how much manual labor and wrist strain could have been spared. That experience sparked an appreciation for smoothing out the level production pipeline and eventually procedural workflows. I became highly motivated to understand procedural scattering as a foundation for asset placement, even though I at this point feel I am still just scratching the surface of what is possible.
Recently, I spent some time testing out various tools and aspects of Houdini for both asset placement and basic mesh generation. Since a friend and I are currently prototyping a game set in a vast desert environment, my goal is to output a lot more content in less time by procedurally distributing rocks and vegetation.

My current plan is to space out larger cliffs in the center, surrounded by smaller intersecting cliffs and finally scattered rocks. The red cubes are automatically placed using a mask created by the curvature, slope, and height of the terrain. The yellow surrounding cubes are then placed using the red cubes as source points. Meanwhile, the purple cubes generate around the initial mask that created the red ones. It is easier to show than tell, so below is a quick video demonstrating the logic.

Naturally the scattering would need to be wastly improved for a real product, more centered around slopes and multiplied by a noise to avoid having the same amount of assets in every slope. But the basic idea is there, and I am excited to continue exploring the possibilities of procedural asset placement.

I also took some time to explore the Labs tools for building trees, and the seem incredibly useful. Below is a setup where a tree generates along a curve.



Furthermore, I spent some time getting re-accustomed to ZBrush and testing out Quixel Mixer for texturing. I definitely prefer this texturing workflow over the manual Photoshop methods I originally learned long ago. I plan to create a few more pieces to use for the cliffs in my experiments.

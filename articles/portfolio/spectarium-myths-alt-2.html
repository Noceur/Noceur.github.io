Spectarium Games

Myths

Senior Technical Level Designer · 2023-2026

I was until recently working at Spectarium Games as a Technical Level Designer on the live-service action RPG 'Myths'. A fast-paced, four-player co-op dungeon crawler where shifting ancient mythologies collide with dynamic, evolving boss encounters. Soft-launched in Early Access 2026.

The bottleneck and the approach

Problem

When I joined the studio, the game was a singleplayer prototype: a single dungeon built in one piece, every enemy placed by hand. We needed to rebuild that for a live-service operation where the player's entire loop sits inside dungeons that take four to six minutes to clear - brutal cadence for a content pipeline.

The main problems were:

  • Art requirements - The wish was to keep the artistical qualities from the prototype for the real product. For the Norse biome this meant long, soft, directional shadows with no dynamic light and no baking in the normal sense, on a mobile light budget.
  • Throughput and Scalability - The production pipeline had to scale better, at the time the pipeline was very manual (decoration, data setup, enemy setup, dungeon assembly) no matter if we employed more level designers we would need to figure out how to build things far more effectively.

Approach

Together with tech and environment art, we converged on a tile-based system. Tiles assembled into zones (the bread-and-butter pieces of a dungeon), zones assembled into dungeons, and each biome carried its own constraints and its own ruleset on top of that.

Every zone went through the same pipeline: blockout → tile layout (plus blockers) → encounters → testing → decoration → custom navmesh → playtest.

Zones, Dungeons, and Tools
Three biomes

Each biome was rooted in a real-world mythology, except for one invented meta-mythology - the Watchers which were sitting on top as a sort of narrative glue. Where the biomes diverged technically was in the tile system that filled the blockout.

Norse

Norse was our first biome and the one which caused us the most headaches. One of the early requirements was that we needed long directional shadows, but neither normal baked nor dynamic light was available for various reasons. The solution was to vertex-paint where the shadows were supposed to fall into the tiles themselves and then take fake the shadow with a shader.
This meant that the environment art team had to slice two extra rows of vertices into each tile (shadow start and shadow end), and since a tile could land in four rotations we needed four authored variants per tile (0°, 90°, 180°, 270°). To keep my sanity I made a small tool auto-swapped tiles to the correct lighting variant as they rotated.

The default tile size was 10x10 units (meters), ideally smaller would have been nicer for better control, however a certain size was required due to the shadow requirement which had to fit into the walls for our cliffside walls. We also had 5x5 open-grass tiles for finer control over wall widths and 20x10 + 20x20 tiles to break up the otherwise easily-spotted grid. Tile types were diagonal, wall, inner-corner, outer-corner and open. Wall colliders always met in the centre of each tile.

Anything that wasn't part of a tile - trees, rocks and other dressing - got a separate floor-shadow object placed on top of the tile. Those could cross zone borders freely, since they had no colliders and didn't affect gameplay.

Mayan

Our second biome was interior and designed to feel claustrophobic after Norse's wide open spaces. We dropped directional light entirely, faked ambient occlusion into the corners, and built zones from floor tiles with worldspace-position texturing so seams between floors disappeared. Walls were placed on top of those floors rather than baked into them.

The new problem here was visibility. The camera couldn't rotate, but interior walls naturally block sightlines. Walls were authored as a low "stub" piece that always indicated where the wall lived, and a tall section on top that could be faded out when the camera needed to see past it. On top of that, two more systems shared the load: a cutout around the player punched through any wall it overlapped, and enemies behind walls were drawn as a red silhouette overlay so they couldn't disappear from the player's read.

Mayan zones.


Watcher

The third biome - and the home of the FTUE. Rules followed Mayan, but we dropped the worldspace-position floor textures so the floors could carry deliberate per-tile patterning with stone tiles of varying sizes (tile-stones, mosaics). To handle the seam where the new floor textures met the walls, we added a trim concept running along that join. Work stopped before the biome - and the game - fully shipped.

Watcher zones.


Special zones

Other than the three biomes, we had a few special zones with their own rulesets. The Archive (the players' base) was built blocked out then entirely decorated by the environment art team, with no tiles at all. Boss zones were also built without tiles, since they needed to break thematically for the encounter and the tile system would have just been a constraint, they also only consisted of a single zone.

Surtr and Camazotz boss zones.

Across the three biomes I shipped around 150 zones. Decoration leaned on compositions (collections of prefabs) that could be dropped into a zone as a unit; the long-term direction was to standardise them per biome so we could swap sub-themes without reauthoring layouts.

Zone structure

Zone types

Zones existed in a handful of types with different functions.
Start zones - The player spawn per dungeon was always empty of enemies.
Regular zones - Carried the bread-and-butter content the majority of players ran through.
Exit zones - Closed out a dungeon and usually held a tougher encounter.
Scenario zones - Were timed event rooms: cursed chests, wave defence, kill-the-spawn-points, or other limited-time objectives for additional rewards.

Adapter zones - Existed to solve the awkward case where two zones wanted to connect but had different-sized exits. Adapters also got to do thematic work: a Mayan dungeon might want narrow side tunnels feeding into a grander structure, so a south→north adapter going small-to-large could put a small "natural" entrance in front and then block that path with rubble, while the actual route reads as the grander gated path beyond - the fake gate is set dressing, the real route opens up into the next zone.

An adapter doing thematic transition work - diverts the player from a small exit to a big entrance in the next zone.

Three meshes per zone

Visual mesh. What the player actually sees. Client-only, with no gameplay role. The server never reads it.

Walkable mesh. Every floor and walkable piece of decoration carried this. The client sampled it to place the player at the correct Y height as they moved; without it players were stuck at y=0 and would walk through the world rather than across it. Client-only.

Drag to swap between the two walkable mesh views.

Navmesh. A custom flat mesh created per zone after deco work was completed. As far as the server was concerned the game was 2D, so the navmesh stayed flat and the stitching algorithm could combine zones cheaply at runtime.

Drag to swap between the two navmesh views.

Zone data (scriptable object)

Each zones gameplay data lived in a scriptable object alongside the meshes.

Enemies - Could either be manually placed as forced spawns (in the FTUE dungeons for example), or handled by a dynamic spawn system that placed them anywhere on the navmesh outside of dynamic-spawn-blocker volumes according to the dungeons set encounter difficulty constraints.

Logic - Ran in two layers. A default layer was always active per zone - things like traps and damage zones that should always be activated were set up here. On top of that, optional scenario graphs could be enabled, with one active at a time. A scenario graph was a node-based object that referenced data fields on the zone, so the same scenario graph could be shared across any zone whose data fields matched the graph's contract.

Exits - Were defined per zone and consumed by the runtime stitching algorithm to piece the navmesh together when a dungeon assembled.

Dungeons - scriptable assembly

A dungeon is a scriptable data object: whether it was a regular or boss dungeon, the list of zones it contained, any scenarios attached to those zones, their positions, and the weather (post-processing effects) for the run.

Assembly improved over the project. At first dungeons were authored by typing zone entries into a list with x/y/z coordinates. Later, a small tool let me drag the zone prefabs into place in a scene, read their positions relative to the starter zone, and emit the scriptable object directly - much easier to keep dungeons reading sensibly thematically when you can visualize them in the scene.

Long-term the majority of these would have been generated from dungeon graph templates - a topic in a later section.

Assembled dungeons: Mayan, Norse, and a Watcher.
Editor tools & macros

I produced over 40 custom tools for this project. Most were wired to a single zone authoring task - snapping, rotation, theme-based automatic tile replacements, controlled per-object randomisation. The rest handled navmesh setup, walkable-mesh setup, zone and dungeon data setup (including batch processing across many objects at once), and validation.

A few of the tools with interface I built for Myths.

With those tools plus a macro keyboard (a tool I think any level designer who value their wrists should have) and a layered scripts setup (swap macro layer depending on task), first-draft zone build time dropped from roughly a day to under an hour (paper design not counted).



For the Norse theme I laid out the creation to correspond to the macro pads button placement on its numpad looking button layout. This meant the each key produced a certain type of tile, wall, corner or one without a blocker. It also had a wheel for undo/redo, one for rotation (which swapped the tile to the correct lighting variant) among other things.

The basic setup process for a zone - visual mesh, walkable mesh, navmesh, and decoration - using the tools and macros described above. Not intended as a quality bar, just a look at the process. Recording itself runs ~5 minutes in real-time; the gameplay data setup on top would add another 3-5 minutes (even for a real example), and dropping it into a dungeon adds roughly another minute.

The Early Access build 2026 ran on three biomes worth of handcrafted zones, hand-assembled dungeons, and a stack of editor tooling that kept the per-zone authoring cost low enough for one person to keep pace with content demand.

Procedural roadmap & reflection

During a company trip 2025 in Prague I held a presentation for the team on procedural dungeon generation on how it could work in Myths. The game was already well into production by then, but the procedural roadmap was still a key topic for the future of the project and the team. I wanted to share my thoughts on how we could approach that, and what the trade-offs were between different approaches.

The idea with the presentation was not to say "this is how we will do it" but rather "here are some options, and here are the trade-offs between them." The version below brings up a few points I brought up without going into details about the constraints we had to deal with.

Dungeon graph
Slide from procedural talk I held for the team in Prague 2025.

The Dungeon graph sits at the top of the level hierarchy and reigns over every other aspect of the dungeon. Each node is a zone with constraints - a theme (or a list of acceptable themes), an encounter density, whether it's optional or mandatory, fixed or varying and the graph randomises order and contents within those rules. This is where partial control lives: force two connected zones to host a scenario, lock a starting biome, guarantee a miniboss appears before an exit, while still letting the rest shuffle (fully randomized or with constraints). Story or scenario beats can be steered from here too.

Decoration hierarchy

Variants

Variants are the big zone-level pieces - a whole wall plus its surroundings, a fancy centerpiece, a pit of gas clouds. The highest level of variable content and where theme control bites: two variants for "ritualistic," two for "ornate," two for "rubble." The dungeon graph picks the theme; the zone picks one of the matching variants.

Large chunks control the majority of a zone's look, varied per theme.

Compositions

Compositions are what variants are mainly built from. We already used them, but the key shift is standardised sizes (S, M, L, XL) with distinct shapes for corners, walls and central standalone pieces. That makes them swappable without trouble: a variant picks "ritual site" with a sacrificial stone, and a randomiser can swap the stone for a statue and shuffle the surrounding props without touching colliders.

Wall and inner-corner compositions, built to slot into the same anchor points.

Micro details

Micro details are the smallest decorative aspects - props of certain kinds, ideally part of a composition or standalone. Randomisable per run with no impact on colliders or navmesh: pebble swaps, bones spawning or not, lanterns on or off. Cheap variety once the setup is in place.

Uncontrolled changes - zero effort after setup.
Result & Reflections

Result

Around 150 zones shipped across three biomes for early access. A 100x100m zone layout could be completed in roughly five minutes thanks to the macro pad plus the auto-tile-swap tooling, with full first-draft zone build time dropping from a day to under an hour. Two of five planned biomes completed end-to-end with their bosses, with the Watcher biome carrying the FTUE. The procedural-content roadmap was fully documented and work had started on it by the time of my departure.

Reflection

The Norse vertex-painted-shadow system was the right answer to the constraints we had, but in hindsight the right move would have been to push back harder on the constraints themselves. I'd also start the dungeon-graph work earlier so the first biome ships on top of it rather than retrofitting later. Additionally standardizing the composition sizes and shapes earlier would have made the later biomes easier to build and set us up better for a procedural workflow.

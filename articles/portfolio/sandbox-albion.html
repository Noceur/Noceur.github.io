I worked at Sandbox Interactive as a Level Designer with technical focus on 'Albion Online'. A PC-first, cross-platform sandbox MMORPG featuring a completely player-driven economy and a classless, gear-based combat system. My work focused on developing highly scalable environments to support both intricate PvE dungeon encounters and massive, full-loot guild wars over territorial control across distinct, resource-driven biomes. Released July 2017.

The monolithic old level system

Albion Online takes place in a single un-instanced world made of hundreds of 1x1km hand-built maps stitched together by loading zones. This means that if the game gets crowded you have to create more land. Great for immersion. Not so great when the playerbase grows tenfold.

The old workflow was that a level designer assembled level by level by manually placing 4x4 unit ground tiles and then filling it up with assets one after another, one click per asset, no painting, no compositions. Throughput was heavily bound by headcount, every map was a one-off or repeated which players noticed.

In very early alpha that was fine. Scope was limited, the audience was small, and peaks sat around 1,000 CCU. Once marketing ramped up during beta and the game grew toward a few thousand concurrent players, the cracks started showing: content demand outpaced what a hand-built per-map workflow could supply, and the repetition got louder the more eyes were on it. Without change launch would be catastrophic.

Old monolithic level setup - everything authored in place.
The new approach

Two systems took the brunt of the rebuild: templates and layers. Templates let levels be assembled out of pre-built chunks instead of from a blank scene; layers let those chunks vary across instances without re-authoring them.

The template stack

The main template was the glue. It defined the silhouette of the level and carried slots for everything else - it didn't hold a lot of gameplay itself but baked in layers for variation and the optional roads underneath.

Main template - the structural backbone of the level.

Border templates defined where exits lived on each side of the map. They also used the layer system so the same border could host different exit configurations.

Border templates - layered exit configurations.

The other spaces in the main template were filled by content templates. This is where the majority of the game actually took place:

  • Monster camps
  • Castles & guild territories
  • Treasures
  • Dungeon entrances
  • Resource hotspots
Content templates - the gameplay-bearing chunks.

On top of all of that, a road system guided players between exits. Roads were layers within the main template, connected by tiny 4x4m crossing templates so the path between any two chosen exits could be assembled cleanly.

The Sheets-driven assembler

Templates and layers solved the authoring problem, but they didn't solve the assembly problem. Stitching templates together still meant moving thousands of objects around in Unity at once, and the editor wasn't happy doing that.

The tool I built side-stepped the editor entirely. It pulled the location of every slot in a main template, the centerpoint of every available content template, and dumped the whole thing into a Google Sheet - one tab for the data, one tab as the assembler UI.

Inside that sheet, the level designer had:

  • Dropdowns per slot, each showing a thumbnail preview of the template.
  • Exit pickers that fed into Dijkstra to compute the road-layer combinations that connect the selected exits.
  • An export button that emitted a level JSON for the Unity project to consume.
Complete templated level - assembled, exported, ready to load.

Result

Level assembly dropped from 15-30 minutes to under a minute, and was substantially less error-prone since the templates and layers carried the constraints rather than relying on the designer to remember them.

By launch the game was peaking over 40,000 CCU, and having the templated workflow in place is what made keeping an audience that size fed with fresh, non-repetitive content actually tractable.

Reflection

The Sheets layer was deliberately temporary - it was the fastest path to a working tool, and it let non-engineers iterate on the assembly experience without code reviews. Long term the natural next step was to fold the same dropdown-and-preview UI into a native Unity editor window, drop the export round-trip, and lean harder into auto-generated road layers.

Asset Mover - the smaller tool that paid off daily

A neighbouring problem: each environmental prop had nine-plus variants (tier x biome) plus their meta files. Renaming or moving one asset meant walking nine folders and touching eighteen files. Artists and designers hated it.

The asset mover let the user select any one of those files and type the new name. The tool returned a preview of the suggested actions across every related variant, the user accepted or rejected, and a log file recorded the final operations. The bulk of the work was a layer of regex that knew which parts of each path were the substitutable bits and which were the load-bearing structural bits.

Level Design

Myths, was the latest project I worked on. Myths is a online ARPG primarily developed for mobile. I was the sole level designer on the project and developed the majority of my own editor toolset to speed up my workflow. I was also responsible for level decoration at first but as the project went on I had to shift focus to solving technical challenges while the environment team took over more and more of the decoration.

The pipeline by the end looked something like this:

  • Level design and layout
  • Gameplay events and encounters
  • Level decoration
  • Navigation mesh

The absolute majority of the game mostly took place in various dungeons which consisted of hand-built rooms (zones) stitched together without loading zones. Each dungeon was designed to have a run time of 4-6 minutes.

Unfortunately due to financial trouble within the company I only got to plan out the majority of the procedural systems but never fulfill my vision. I will include those plans at the end of this section.

The plan was to have 5 biomes at launch with a end boss for each, at the time of me leaving the project we had 3 biomes and 2 bosses in various stages of completion, the Norse and Mayan biomes were the most complete while the Watcher biome was still in middle of development.

I had close collaboration with tech but especially environment art. Together with a fantastic env art (and tech art) team we solved the setup for 3 biomes with vastly different and often hard to hit requirements. For norse we "baked" the shadows into the vertex colors and with directional (but static) light that meant we were forced to have a very specific setup for the tiles as they were directional. Rotating a tile meant swapping out the mesh for another just for the sake of the light. We also had to use various other creative solutions for light and shadows. In retrospect this was not the best solution. for the Mayan biome we had more freedom but still had to be careful with how we set up the levels to make sure the lighting looked good and for the Watcher biome we had a more dynamic lighting setup which meant we had a lot more freedom but also had to be careful with how we set up the levels to make sure the lighting looked good. The biomes had different defining charactaristics which meant we couldn't just swap one biome for another to increase our level pool. Norse was outdoors with directional (but static) light, it had wide open spaces and often smaller branching paths. Mayan was our first indoor biome where we finally tried incorporating verticallity to some degree. The server only knew of one height level so verticallity was mostly an illusion. We also tried to have more small rooms and narrow passages. We also tried to not have a directional light setup which gave us a lot more freedom. The Watchers was our last biome was also indoors but it had wider areas, with a lot more fake light. To add:
Company ran out of money, if it had continued the plans for the game was as following: Destructibles, Traps, Dungeon generation graph. Unfortunately it burned before then.

Procedural strategy

A while back I put together a presentation for the team laying out a plan for how to shift our dungeon content from fully hand-built to a more procedural workflow. The original was meant to spark a discussion, so it leans into tradeoffs rather than absolutes — the version below pulls those thoughts together as a written walkthrough of how I approach scaling content delivery for a live-service game.

The problem

A fully manual pipeline produced nice-looking zones but didn't scale. Three issues kept surfacing.

The first is repetition: players churn through dungeons quickly, and any obvious reuse is noticed within minutes. With every zone made by hand, output is gated by how many people we hire. The second is value per time invested: when a zone is reused as-is, the second pass returns the same content the player already saw — output stays 1:1 with authoring effort. The third is plain workflow. Layouts, decoration, encounters and dungeon assembly are all manual; we have tools that ease parts of it, but the wrist pain is real and the throughput is too low. I would rather generate content than generating carpal tunnel issues.



Two paths

Two directions came out of this, both valid with different tradeoffs.

Variants are the safer bet given our constraints. A natural extension of what we already have, it retains current quality levels and squeezes a lot of additional value out of each zone for a comparatively small amount of extra work. This is the path I'd push for, and most of what follows is about how it would work.

Fully generated is the alternative — generating rooms or dungeons in one go. More rigid, more upfront investment, but huge long-term value across the project and likely future ones. Worth prototyping in parallel for parts of the game rather than betting the project on it now.

Dungeon graph

The graph sits at the top of the hierarchy and rains down over every other aspect of the dungeon. Each node is a zone with constraints — a theme (or a list of acceptable themes), an encounter density, whether it's optional or mandatory, fixed or varying — and the graph randomizes order and contents within those rules. This is where partial control lives: we can force two connected zones to host a scenario, lock down a starting biome, or guarantee a miniboss appears before an exit, while still letting the rest shuffle. Story or scenario aspects can be steered from here too.

Building zones

Setting up layouts and navmesh is the smaller part of building a zone, so I'd skip looking into automating that for now. Navmesh authoring itself is straightforward but error-prone — small misses compound and there are a lot of edge cases. It is also worth a separate look at how we build and store them (more on that under technical considerations further down). Norse and Mayan biomes also developed slightly different conventions — Mayan with freestanding rotatable walls, Norse directional — and we should decide whether we are keeping both for interior versus exterior or unifying.

Decoration hierarchy

Decoration is where most of the time goes, alongside chasing elusive navmesh issues. Right now everything lives as compositions or freestanding props, which is too flat. I'd split it into three layers, each with a different level of control.

Variants

Variants are the big zone-level pieces — a whole wall plus its surroundings, a fancy centerpiece, a pit of gas clouds. This is the highest level of variable content and where theme control bites: we might have two variants for "ritualistic," two for "ornate," two for "rubble." The dungeon graph picks the theme, the zone picks one of its matching variants.

Large chunks control the majority of a zone's look, varied per theme.





Compositions

Compositions are what variants are mainly built from. We already use them, but the key shift is standardized sizes (S, M, L, XL) with distinct shapes for corners, walls and central standalone pieces. That makes them swappable without trouble: a variant picks "ritual site" with a sacrificial stone, and a randomizer can swap the stone for a statue and shuffle the surrounding props, bigger pieces and all, without touching colliders. I still think we should keep some free, unconstrained compositions around — they have their place — but the bulk should be sized and shaped on purpose.

Wall and inner-corner compositions, built to slot into the same anchor points.


Outer-corner and freestanding variants, swappable for each other at the right size class.



Micro details

Micro details are our smallest decorative aspects — props of certain kinds, ideally part of a composition or standalone. They can be randomized per run with no impact on colliders or navmesh: pebble swaps, bones spawning or not, lanterns on or off. Cheap variety once the setup is in place. Freestanding props don't go away under this scheme, just used more sparingly.

Uncontrolled changes — zero effort after setup.





Background and foreground are basically unsolved today. For backgrounds I lean toward vistas built from chunks that can be slapped together (and randomized) rather than continuous layers running the full length of the dungeon. Foreground I haven't had time to consider properly — open question.

Encounters

Hand-placed encounters carry little value for our combat style and break easily: designers update visuals without re-checking enemies, and there is no zone editor that shows both at once. The better default is to auto-spread enemy clusters across each zone and keep directed combat for scenarios. Bosses and tutorials will still be manual — those don't go away, and the same applies to scenarios once we have them.

Auto-spread enemy clusters with directed encounters reserved for scenarios and bosses.



Events

Three tiers, each with different control.

Scenarios are fixed zones with manual setup — objects players interact with to trigger a challenge and earn a reward. Some can share a zone visually, and the dungeon graph lets us force two of them to land in connected zones.

Minor gameplay events are algorithm-driven: a random enemy ambush, a treasure-goblin spawn. Traps probably live here too, though I haven't placed them yet.

Visual events are flavor moments with no mechanical impact — dust raining from a rubble pile as a player approaches, a squirrel crossing the path, the shadow of something large rushing across the screen. Most likely client-side.

Fully generated dungeons

The alternative path, briefly. I don't have a fixed proposal here — likely some grid-based algorithm, possibly anchored wave function collapse. It is exciting and clearly the long-term lever: a system like this lends itself to future projects too. It is also more rigid — a balancing act between artistic freedom and systematically producing dungeons en masse — and with enough control and thought you can get genuinely beautiful results out of it. Path of Exile is a good example. Worth exploring at a later point with some light prototyping to see what comes out.

Technical considerations

A few loose threads — they don't fit neatly into the plan above but they shape what is actually feasible.

Navmesh and colliders are part of the game config and a good chunk of why a build takes time. Could we checksum them and skip rebuilds when unchanged?

Level data — if we end up with 500 zones and 10,000 levels, do we hit a storage or distribution wall? Storing seeds instead of fully generated data might be worth it.

Dungeon pool — tied to the previous point. If storage really is a problem, we could rotate a pool of dungeons every X days.

Zone editing tool — needed to inspect gameplay setup alongside zone geometry. Am I overlapping a gameplay event with a collider? Is a monster or boss getting stuck in a wall? Hard to answer without the tool.

Eval and QA — more randomization makes QA harder. We would likely need to generate alongside a vetting process before pushing dungeons live.

Pipeline

Most of my tools were small scripts to speed up my workflow. Snapping, rotating, automatically swapping out tiles to other ones with different lighting baked in etc. I also set up a zone setup tool that created all necessary meta data required and similarly for the dungeons. With some custom scripts and setups I was able to set up the tiles and layout of a 100x100m zone in roughly 5 minutes.

+

Macros

For the sake of efficiency I also worked with a layered macro pad for my most used tools and shortcuts which I would strongly recommend for any level designer who want to spare their hands.

Spectarium Games

Myths

Senior Technical Level Designer · 2023-2026

I was until recently working at Spectarium Games as a Technical Level Designer on the live-service action RPG 'Myths'. A fast-paced, four-player co-op dungeon crawler where shifting ancient mythologies collide with dynamic, evolving boss encounters. Soft-launched in Early Access 2026.

Initial Challenges

When i joined, the game was a single player prototype: a single dungeon, built in one pieces, every enemy placed by hand. For a life service operation, this had to be rebuilt. The goal was a game loop of four to six minutes per dungeon and not too much repetition - brutal cadence for a content pipeline.

The main challenges:

  • Art direction pushed to keep the artistic qualities of prototype for the release. For one biome, this meant long, soft, directional shadows with no dynamic light or traditional baking methods baking. And all that on a mobile light budget.
  • As for throughput and scalability, we were working with a very manual pipeline. Decoration, data set up, enemy setup and dungeon assembly was all done by hand - completely unsustainable considering the scale of the project.

The approach

I worked closely with the tech and environment art teams to design a tile-based system. Tiles assembled into zones which in turn assembled into dungeons.

The pipeline I decided upon was: for the zone creation itself was: blockout → tile layout (plus blockers) → encounters → testing → decoration → custom navmesh → playtest. Depending on my workload I could hand over the decoration step to the environment art team, and take over once that was done for some manual adjustments and continuing with the navmesh work.

Zones, Dungeons, and Tools
Three biomes

Each biome was rooted in a real-world mythology, except for one invented meta-mythology - the Watchers which were sitting on top as a sort of narrative glue. Where the biomes diverged technically was in the tile system that filled the blockout.

Norse

Norse was our first biome and the one which caused us the most headaches. One of the early requirements was that we needed long directional shadows, but neither baked lighting nor dynamic lighting solutions were available for various reasons. The fix was to vertex-paint where the shadows were supposed to fall into the tiles themselves and then fake the shadow with a shader.
This meant that the environment art team had to slice two extra rows of vertices into each tile (shadow start and shadow end), and since a tile could land in four rotations we needed four authored variants per tile (0°, 90°, 180°, 270°). To keep my sanity I made a small tool that auto-swapped tiles to the correct lighting variant as they rotated.

The default tile size was 10x10 units (meters) - ideally smaller would have been nicer for better control, but the shadows cast by our cliff side walls needed to fit onto the tile's ground. We also had 5x5 open-grass tiles for finer control over wall widths and 20x10 + 20x20 tiles to break up the otherwise easily-spotted grid. Tile types were diagonal, wall, inner-corner, outer-corner and open. Wall colliders always met in the centre of each tile.

Anything that wasn't part of a tile - trees, rocks and other dressings - got a separate floor-shadow object placed on top of the tile. Those could cross zone borders freely, since they had no colliders and didn't affect gameplay.

Mayan

Our second biome was interior focused, and designed to feel claustrophobic after Norse's wide open spaces. We dropped directional light entirely, faked ambient occlusion into the corners, and built zones from floor tiles with worldspace-position texturing so seams between floors disappeared. Walls were placed on top of those floors rather than baked into them.

The new problem here was visibility. The camera couldn't rotate, but interior walls naturally block sightlines. Walls were authored as a low "stub" piece that always indicated where the wall lived, and a tall section on top that could be faded out when the camera needed to see past it. On top of that, two more systems shared the load: a cutout around the player punched through any wall it overlapped, and enemies behind walls were drawn as a red silhouette overlay so they couldn't disappear from the player's read.

Mayan zones.


Watcher

The third biome - and the home of the First Time User Experience. Rules followed Mayan, but we dropped the worldspace-position floor textures so the floors could carry deliberate per-tile patterning with stone tiles of varying sizes.. To handle the seam where the new floor textures met the walls, we added a trim concept running along that join. Work stopped before the biome - and the game - fully shipped.

Watcher zones.


Special zones

Other than the three biomes, we had a few special zones with their own rulesets. The Archive (the players' base) was built blocked out then entirely decorated by the environment art team, with no tiles at all. Boss zones were also built without tiles, since they needed to break thematically for the encounter, so the tile system would have just been a constraint. They also only consisted of a single zone.

Across the three biomes I shipped around 150 zones. Decoration leaned on compositions (collections of prefabs) that could be dropped into a zone as a unit; the long-term direction was to standardise them per biome so we could swap sub-themes without reauthoring layouts.

Zone structure

Zone types

Zones exist in a handful of types with different functions.
Start zones - The start zone of the dungeon, always enemy free.
Regular zones - Carries the bread-and-butter of the content which the players runs through.
Exit zones - Closes out a dungeon and usually held a tougher encounter.
Scenario zones - Are timed event rooms: cursed chests, wave defence, kill-the-spawn-points, or other limited-time objectives for additional rewards.

Adapter zones - Existed to solve the awkward case where two zones wanted to connect but had different-sized exits. Adapters also got to do thematic work: a Mayan dungeon might want narrow side tunnels feeding into a grander structure, so a south→north adapter going small-to-large could put a small "natural" entrance in front and then block that path with rubble, while the actual route reads as the grander gated path beyond - the fake gate is set dressing, the real route opens up into the next zone.

An adapter doing thematic transition work - diverts the player from a small exit to a big entrance in the next zone.

Three meshes per zone

Visual mesh. What the player actually sees. Client-only, with no gameplay role. The server never reads it.

Walkable mesh. Every floor and walkable piece of decoration carried this. The client sampled it to place the player at the correct Y height as they moved; without it players were stuck at y=0 and would walk through the world rather than across it. Client-only.

Drag to swap between the two walkable mesh views.

Navmesh. A custom flat mesh created per zone after deco work was completed. As far as the server was concerned the game was 2D, so the navmesh stayed flat and the stitching algorithm could combine zones cheaply at runtime.

Drag to swap between the two navmesh views.

Zone data (scriptable object)

Each zones gameplay data lived in a scriptable object alongside the meshes.

Enemies - Could either be manually placed as forced spawns (in the FTUE dungeons for example), or handled by a dynamic spawn system that placed them anywhere on the navmesh outside of dynamic-spawn-blocker volumes according to the dungeons set encounter difficulty constraints.

Logic - Ran in two layers. A default layer was always active per zone - things like traps and damage zones that should always be activated were set up here. On top of that, optional scenario graphs could be enabled, with one active at a time. A scenario graph was a node-based object that referenced data fields on the zone, so the same scenario graph could be shared across any zone whose data fields matched the graph's contract.

Exits - Were defined per zone and consumed by the runtime stitching algorithm to piece the navmesh together when a dungeon assembled.

Dungeons - scriptable assembly

A dungeon is a scriptable data object: whether it was a regular or boss dungeon, the list of zones it contained, any scenarios attached to those zones, their positions, and the weather (post-processing effects) for the run.

Assembly improved as time went on. At first dungeons were authored by typing zone entries into a list with x/y/z coordinates. Later, a small tool let me drag the zone prefabs into place in a scene, read their positions relative to the starter zone, and emit the scriptable object directly - much easier to keep dungeons reading sensibly thematically when you can visualize them in the scene.

Long-term the majority of these would have been generated from dungeon graph templates - a topic in a later section.

Assembled dungeons: Mayan, Norse, and a Watcher.
Editor tools & macros

I produced over 40 custom tools for this project. Most were wired to a single zone authoring task - snapping, rotation, theme-based automatic tile replacements, controlled per-object randomisation. The rest handled navmesh setup, walkable-mesh setup, zone and dungeon data setup (including batch processing across many objects at once), and validation.

A few of the tools with interface I built for Myths.

With those tools plus a macro keyboard (a device I recommend to any level designer who valuest their wrists) and a layered scripts setup (swap macro layer depending on task), first-draft zone build time dropped from roughly a day to under an hour.

Procedural roadmap & reflection

During a company trip 2025 to Prague, I gave a presentation on procedural dungeon generation and how it could work for Myths. The game was already well into production by then, but the procedural roadmap was still a key topic for the future of the project and the team. I wanted to share my thoughts on how we could tackle this, and what the trade-offs were between different approaches. The version below brings up a few points I brought up without going into details about the constraints we had to deal with.

Dungeon graph
Slide from procedural talk I held for the team in Prague 2025.

The Dungeon graph sits at the top of the level hierarchy and reigns over every other aspect of the dungeon. Each node is a zone with constraints - a theme (or a list of acceptable themes), an encounter density, whether it's optional or mandatory, fixed or varying and the graph randomises order and contents within those rules. This is where partial control lives: force two connected zones to host a scenario, lock a starting biome, guarantee a miniboss appears before an exit, while still letting the rest shuffle (fully randomized or with constraints). Story or scenario beats can be steered from here too.

Decoration hierarchy

Variants

Variants are the biggest zone-level changes - It acts as layers and could be a whole wall plus its surroundings, a fancy centerpiece, a pit of gas clouds. A Mayan zone might have two variants for "ritualistic," two for "ornate," two for "rubble." The dungeon graph picks the theme; the zone picks one of the matching variants.

Large chunks control the majority of a zone's look, varied per theme.

Compositions

Compositions are what variants are mainly built from. We already used them, but the key shift is standardised sizes (S, M, L, XL) with distinct shapes to fit corners, walls and central standalone pieces. That makes them swappable without trouble: The variant choosen for a specific zone through the dungeon graph could be "ritual site" with a sacrificial stone. A randomiser (based on the dungeon seed) could then swap the ritual stone composition for a statue composition and shuffle the surrounding props without affecting the zones navmesh.

Wall and inner-corner compositions, built to slot into the same anchor points.

Micro details

Micro details are the smallest decorative aspects - props of certain kinds, ideally part of a composition or standalone. Randomisable per run with no impact on colliders or navmesh: pebble swaps, bones spawning or not, lanterns on or off. Cheap variety once the setup is in place.

Uncontrolled changes - zero effort after setup.
Result & Reflections

Result

Around 150 zones shipped across three biomes for early access. A 100x100m zone layout could be completed in roughly five minutes thanks to the macro pad plus the auto-tile-swap tooling, with full first-draft zone build time dropping from a day to under an hour. This meant zone creation cost was low enough that a single person could keep pace with content demand. Three biomes were completed together with two bosses and unique arenas, with the Watcher biome carrying the FTUE. The procedural-content roadmap was fully documented and work had started on it by the time of my departure.

Reflection

The Norse vertex-painted-shadow system was the right answer to the constraints we had, but in hindsight the right move would have been to push back harder on the constraints themselves. I'd also start the dungeon-graph work earlier so the first biome ships on top of it rather than retrofitting later. Additionally standardizing the composition sizes and shapes earlier would have made the later biomes easier to build and set us up better for a procedural workflow.
